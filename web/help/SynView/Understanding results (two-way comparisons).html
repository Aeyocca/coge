I have results!  What do they mean?
 
To understand the output from SynView, let's break down the image into its parts.  First, let's take a look at the graphical representation of one genomic region:
Genomic regions are broken into two halves -- the top half for the top strand of duplexed DNA and the bottom half for the bottom strand of DNA.  The dashed line in the middle represents the division between the top and bottom strand.  
 
Genomic features, such as gene models, are drawn closest to the center dashed line and usually are drawn as arrows point in the 5'->3' direction.  Thus, genes on the top and bottom strand will be pointing to the right and left respectively.  In the example shown above, gene models are drawn as a set of overlapping arrows.  The gene is the colored red arrow, on top of which is drawn the mRNA (blue arrow), on top of which is drawn CDS (coding) regions (green or yellow arrow).  In the above example, you will notice that one gene has its CDS regions colored yellow instead of green.  This is because this gene (At3g09690) was used to retrieve the genomic region and was highlighted yellow to provide a visual cue for identifying it quickly.  Also, you will notice that unlike the other genes in this region, this gene is represented by two sets of arrows instead of only one.  This is because this gene has a transcript splice variant.  When two genomic features overlap on the same strand, they are drawn (at least in this example) in such a way as to show both features.  This is achieved by dividing the height of the pictorial representation of the genomic features by the number of overlapping features.  So, although this works well for a small number of overlapping features, it can become difficult to visualize if more than 5 features overlap the same region.  You can turn on and off this behavior in the "Image Parameters".
 
Blast HSPs generated by bl2seq are displayed as colored, numbered boxes.  In the above example, they are blue boxes with a number inside.  The number is their HSP number and is used to identify the corresponding partner of the HSP in another genomic region.  If the HSP is in the "++" orientation, it are drawn on the top strand, and if the HSP is in the "+-" orientation, it is drawn on the bottom strand.  In the above example, all the HSPs are in the "++" orientation and are drawn on the top strand.
 
And finally, the chromosomal region you have chosen in displayed at the top.  In the above example, the gene used to identify the genomic region is show along with its organism of origin and the amount of upstream and downstream sequence used.  
 
Now, let's go back and look at the results for a two region comparison.  If you look at the two genes that highlighted in yellow, you will notice that they have a series of HSPs that are in the same order (from left to right, HSPs: 13, 14, 12, 15, 11, 2, 8, 5, 6, 4, 10).  This tells us a couple of interesting things.  The first is that these two genes share a high degree of sequence similarity in their coding regions (HSPs: 11, 2, 8, 5, 6, 4, 10) and, as such, these genes are highly likely to be homologs (paralogs to be exact.)  Next, they share a series of HSPs in non-coding sequence (HSPs: 13, 14, 12, 15) and the order of these HSPs is conserved.  These are conserved non-coding sequences (CNSs) and are assumed to have some function because their sequences have been conserved since the divergence of these two genes.  In plants, CNSs are likely to bind regulatory proteins, and are unlikely to either encode or bind small RNAs.  (Thomas et al. 2007).
Next, you will notice that there is a series of genes with a similar HSP pattern:  high degree of sequence similarity in coding sequences.  In the above picture, these HSPs are grouped together with blue ovals to highlight the sets.  Each set has a corresponding set in the other genomic region and indicates that the genes underlying these pairs of HSP sets are very likely to be paralogs.  Now, since we can see that there are a series of paralogous genes that also show a conservation of gene order in their respective genomic regions, we have probably identified a region of synteny.  (Synteny is evidence that two or more genomic regions are descended from a common ancestral genomic region.)

Help!  I'm lost in the results!  What can I do to get more information?
 
Although SynView has been designed to represent comparative genomic data in an easy to digest visual format, it is still easy to be overwhelmed by the amount of information returned from SynView -- especially when large genomic regions are compared (such as the 400kb vs 400kb example in the above image.)  To get more information about a particular genomic feature of HSP simply mouse-over it and any associated information will be displayed in the information box.  If you want more information about an HSP, simply click on it and a window will pop-up with more information (see image below).  Also, we are currently working on new methods for visualizing larger chromosomal regions to make analyses easier.  A few options to look forward to in future releases of SynView is automatic highlighting of contiguous HSPs that overlap an existing gene model or other genomic annotation, and dynamic highlighting of genomic regions linked by an HSP.
